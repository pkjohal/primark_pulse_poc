## Opening / Login

This demo is a walk through Primark Pulse — an application that replaces all the radios, paper checklists, WhatsApp groups, and manual spreadsheets that store teams currently juggle, and puts everything in one mobile app that every colleague carries on the shop floor.

To begin, Sarah opens the application. The login screen has been designed for the shop floor: select your store, select your name from the list, enter a four-digit PIN. That's it — no email address, no forgotten passwords.

---

## Slide 1 — Store Overview (Home)

This is Sarah's home screen — her operational command centre for the shift. Before she's spoken to a single colleague, she knows exactly what's happening across the store.

At the top right you can see the status badge — right now we're on Amber, which means the store needs attention. That badge is calculated automatically from real data: critical jobs, compliance progress, and open stock issues all feed into it. Green means all good, red means action required.

Below the greeting there's an AI-generated suggestion. The system has spotted that the queue at Main Tills is building ahead of the lunch rush, and it's recommending Sarah move two colleagues from the Stockroom. The explanation is right there — she doesn't have to figure out why, just decide whether she agrees. She can act on it with one tap or dismiss it and move on.

The four cards give her the live pulse of the store: nine colleagues on the floor, two open jobs are CRITICAL, stock alerts flagged, and compliance sitting at around fifty percent — the closing checklist hasn't been started yet.

At the bottom, the queue summary shows every monitored area. Fitting Rooms is already over threshold — twelve people waiting against a limit of eight. That's going to need attention.

---

## Slide 2 — My Schedule

Now let's look at Sarah's personal schedule. The schedule page shows her full working week at a glance. Today's shift has a progress bar — she's about a third of the way through — and the system knows her break time, her assigned zone, and her finish time without her having to check anything.

The shift swap feature handles something that currently happens entirely through text messages and manager approval chains. If Sarah needs Friday off, she taps Offer for Swap and the shift immediately shows as available to anyone in the same store with the same role. A colleague who wants extra hours can accept it — one tap, both schedules update, the manager gets visibility.

---

## Slide 3 — Staff Overview

The staff overview page shows where the colleague is right now, whether they're active, on break, or absent, and what shift they're working. The colour-coded status dots mean you can scan the whole roster in a second.

And if she needs to move someone — acting on that AI suggestion from the home screen and pulling someone from the Stockroom to the Tills — she just taps their card, hits Reallocate, picks the new zone, and it's done.

---

## Slide 4 — Team Comms

Team communications in most stores run through WhatsApp or radio, which means critical information gets lost in group chat noise and there's no way to know if everyone has actually read it.

Here, Sarah has sent a fire drill announcement and it sits at the top of the feed with an URGENT badge. But the key thing is the acknowledgment tracker — she can see in real time how many colleagues have confirmed they've seen it. That's accountability. If she sends a policy update that everyone needs to read before opening, she knows exactly who has and who hasn't — and can chase the ones who haven't.

## Slide 5 — Job Management

Every job in the store is here. The list is sorted by priority — CRITICAL at the top, each one showing a *Why it Matters* reason, the zone, and a live SLA timer that turns amber then red as time runs out.

At the top, the Today's Top Jobs carousel has already done the triage — it scores jobs against priority, SLA urgency, and time of day, and surfaces the three that need attention right now.

Let's open this first one — 'Restock jeans wall, Slim Fit range.' The detail sheet shows the full picture: why it matters, the success criteria so the colleague knows exactly when they're done, and a peer tip from Store 42. That institutional knowledge would normally walk out the door with whoever discovered it.

Sarah can create a new job from scratch using the button at the top — she sets the title, priority, zone, and time to complete, adds a reason and success criteria, and assigns it directly to a colleague from the staff picker. One form, no radio.

For this existing job, she hits Assign — the system shows active staff with their skills and current zone. One tap, job is assigned, SLA clock running, colleague sees it immediately.

---

## Slide 6 — Queues & Demand

The Queues page closes the loop on everything we've seen so far. This is the real-time demand picture for the store.

At the top, the store pressure indicator gives a forward-looking view. Right now we're at Medium, but the system is flagging that a peak is expected in about forty-five minutes when the lunch rush hits. That's not reactive — that's proactive. It gives Sarah a window to act before customers start queuing out the door.

The individual queue cards show each monitored area. Main Tills are fine — eight people, threshold is ten. But Fitting Rooms are already over threshold — twelve people waiting, threshold is eight. That red fill bar is the visual signal that something needs to happen.

And here's where it all connects: that's the exact trigger the AI on the home screen was responding to. It saw this queue, saw that colleagues were available in the Stockroom, and surfaced a specific recommendation to Sarah. The system isn't just showing data — it's joining the dots and telling you what to do about it.

---

## Slide 7 — Scan Stock

Now let's say Sarah's had a customer ask for size 32 jeans in Dark Blue. Normally she'd have to walk to the stockroom, check the system on a shared computer, then walk back. With Pulse, she does it right here.

The scan page opens straight to the camera scanner. If we were on device, you'd just hold it up to any barcode. Let me type one in to keep things moving — this is the barcode for the Slim Fit Stretch Jeans we saw on that CRITICAL job a moment ago.

Instantly: product name, price, category, size and colour. And then the numbers that actually matter on the shop floor — seven in this store, fourteen at the nearby Oxford Street store, and forty-three at the distribution centre. If the size the customer wants isn't here, Sarah can tell them in thirty seconds whether another store has it.

More useful still — the floor location. Zone B, Aisle 4, Bay 3. No more vague 'try the back of menswear' — it's the exact shelf, right on screen.

---

## Slide 8 — Stock & Availability

The stock overview gives Sarah a broader picture of what's available across the store and where shortfalls exist. She can see at a glance which zones have stock issues flagged and drill into specific lines.

If she spots that stock is low on a line, she can add it to the replenishment basket right now, set the quantity, and submit the request — all without leaving the shop floor or logging into a separate system. The basket persists even if she closes the app and comes back later.

If something looks wrong — say the stock count doesn't match what's physically on the shelf — she can report a stock issue and flag it for investigation with a single tap.

---

## Slide 9 — Compliance

Compliance is one of the areas where manual processes create the most risk. Closing checklists done on paper get lost, signatures get skipped, issues go unreported. Pulse turns this into a structured, auditable digital process.

You can see the opening checklist was completed earlier this morning by Sarah — timestamped, all items done, signature captured. That's a permanent record, instantly accessible. Now let's look at the closing checklist — it's not started yet, which matches what we saw on the dashboard.

When Sarah starts it, the items are organised into sections and each one has the right input type built in. Some are simple yes/no ticks. Some require a number — like confirming the till float — with validation so you can't enter something out of range. Some require a photo.

If Sarah spots something during the check — say a fire exit is partially blocked — she can flag it immediately, right on the item, with a severity level and a description. That gets captured, tracked, and visible without anyone needing to remember to mention it at handover.

Once all the required items are done, Sarah signs off digitally — on screen, right here. The signature is stored against the checklist record. There's an unbroken chain of accountability.

And if she's not sure about a policy — 'Can I exchange without a receipt?' — the policy search feature lets her ask in plain English and get an answer drawn from Primark's own SOPs, with a source citation so she can verify if she needs to.

---

## Slide 10 — Performance Insights

The Performance Insights page gives Sarah a summary view across the shift and the week. Key operational metrics — compliance completion rates, job resolution times, queue incidents — are tracked here, giving her the evidence she needs for handover notes, shift debriefs, and escalations.

Rather than reconstructing what happened from memory at the end of a shift, she has a timestamped record of every action taken. That's the kind of data that turns a subjective conversation into an objective one.

---

## Slide 11 — Summary / Close

That's Primark Pulse — one app, one shift, end to end.

To recap what we've seen: Sarah starts her shift with an instant picture of the whole store — jobs, staff, queues, compliance — all in one place, with AI surfacing what needs attention first. She assigns work, checks stock, monitors queues, runs a compliance checklist, and keeps the team informed, all without leaving the app or going back to a desktop. And throughout all of it, there's a complete digital record — who did what, when, and why.

The shift from paper and radio to something like this isn't just about convenience. It's about accountability, speed, and not losing the institutional knowledge that walks out the door every time someone leaves.

## Key Themes to Return to

These are the five ideas that run through the whole demo. If you get a question or have a quiet moment, anchor back to one of these:

**One app.** Everything that currently lives across radios, paper, WhatsApp, and shared desktops — in one place.

**Role-aware.** The same app gives a completely different experience to a manager, a floor lead, and a store colleague. Relevant by default.

**AI with explanations.** Every AI suggestion says *why* it's recommending something. It's not a black box — it's a colleague making a suggestion you can agree with or override.

**Nothing gets lost.** Timestamped checklists, digital signatures, acknowledgment tracking, job history. A permanent record of every shift, every decision.

**Knowledge that stays.** Peer tips on jobs, policy search, shift history. The things that walk out the door when someone leaves — captured and shared.
